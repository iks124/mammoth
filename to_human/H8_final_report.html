2. Two Critical Findings

Finding A: The "Zero-Order Trap" (H7)

Flaw 5 Confirmed: Preserving loss on one batch ≠ preserving training trajectory.
The LoRA teleportation optimizes: total_loss = lt_weight * L_t − cos_sim + reg * lora_reg Where L_t = |L(θ + ΔΘ, batch) − L_ref| is the loss invariance constraint. This creates a two-sided trap with no viable middle ground:

3. Why LoRA Teleportation Cannot Work (Theory)

Flaw 1: cos_sim ≠ Forgetting Reduction
Improving gradient alignment on one batch at one instant doesn't guarantee the training trajectory will avoid forgetting. 3130 steps accumulate tiny errors that overwhelm any momentary improvement.
Flaw 4: 512-dimensional search space
Rank-2 LoRA on last 2 layers = 512 free parameters. The conflict manifold lives in a much higher-dimensional space. The LoRA subspace cannot always find solutions.
Flaw 5: Zero-order guarantee ≠ trajectory (CONFIRMED)
L_t = 0 at one batch means ΔΘ is large (25–30 norm). This large delta perturbs ALL other batches. The training trajectory is disrupted worse than doing nothing.
Root cause: Conflict is pervasive (H8)
If 79% of steps have conflict, no teleportation strategy applied to a subset of steps can systematically change the gradient conflict character of training.

5. What We Learned

  1. Gradient conflict (cos_sim < 0) is the normal state of CL training, not an exceptional event. 79% of steps show conflict. You cannot "fix" forgetting by correcting 1% of steps.
  2. Loss invariance at one batch ≠ trajectory invariance. The zero-order constraint is fundamentally insufficient — it forces large ΔΘ that disrupts all other batches.
  3. Task-boundary teleport is neutral at best (−0.15%). The initial gradient is too noisy a proxy for 3130-step trajectory direction.
  4. freq=1 is never viable (12× slowdown, −31.6% accuracy). The compute cost of online second-order gradients is prohibitive.
  5. The fundamental hypothesis is not confirmable with mini-batch cos_sim. The proxy lacks discriminating power (0.17 unit range, 12 data points).

6. Directions NOT Pursued (and Why)

  • SAM-style first-order approximation (Option C): Would reduce compute but doesn't address Flaw 5. The structural issue remains.
  • Conditional merge (Option D): Only ~5% of events pass strict criteria. Too few merges to systematically reduce forgetting over 3130 steps.
  • Higher-rank LoRA: Rank-2 is already expensive (rank-2 × 2 states × 2 passes = 4 second-order forward passes). Higher rank would be 4–8× slower.

7. Conclusion

The LoRA teleportation approach for continual learning does not work in this form. This is a coherent, rigorous negative result supported by:
  • 8 hypothesis families, 15+ experiments
  • Structural analysis (5 identified flaws, 2 confirmed experimentally)
  • Hypothesis verification experiment (H8) showing the fundamental premise is not supported
The problem is not hyperparameters — it is the design itself.
